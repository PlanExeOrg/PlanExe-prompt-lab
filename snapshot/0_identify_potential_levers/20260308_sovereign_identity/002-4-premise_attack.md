### Premise Attack 1 — Integrity
_Forensic audit of foundational soundness across axes._

**[STRATEGIC] A €1.4M project to influence AltID procurement toward platform neutrality is unlikely to succeed given the existing MitID's deep integration with Apple/Google and the likely prioritization of user convenience over resilience by Danish authorities.**

**Bottom Line:** REJECT: The project's premise of significantly influencing AltID procurement toward platform neutrality is unrealistic given the existing MitID's deep integration with Apple/Google, the likely prioritization of user convenience, and the limited resources available to counter the influence of major technology companies.


#### Reasons for Rejection

- The project's success hinges on influencing AltID procurement, but the existing MitID system, deeply entrenched with Apple/Google, creates significant inertia against platform-neutral alternatives.
- The project's reliance on 'resilience framing, procurement intervention, standards pressure, and credible technical demonstration' is unlikely to overcome the convenience and cost advantages of the existing Apple/Google-dependent system.
- The project's success criteria include 'inclusion of platform-neutrality, fallback access, continuity, resilience, or supplier-concentration language in at least one official AltID-related design, policy, consultation, or procurement document,' which is a low bar that doesn't guarantee actual implementation or impact.
- The project's budget of 10.5 million DKK over 24 months is insufficient to overcome the lobbying power and technical resources of Apple and Google, who have a vested interest in maintaining their dominance in the digital identity space.
- The project's reliance on a coalition of civil-society organizations, privacy advocates, academic researchers, and security experts may not be sufficient to counter the influence of established industry players and government priorities focused on user experience and cost.

#### Second-Order Effects

- 0–6 months: The project will likely produce a feasibility report and technical demonstrators, but these may be dismissed by Danish authorities as impractical or too costly to implement.
- 1–3 years: AltID will likely proceed with a design that maintains significant dependence on Apple and Google, despite the project's advocacy efforts.
- 5–10 years: Denmark's digital identity infrastructure will remain vulnerable to the policies and actions of Apple and Google, potentially leading to disruptions in access to essential services.

#### Evidence

- Case/Incident — NHS Digital Identity Programme (2022): The UK's attempt to create a national digital identity system faced significant challenges due to privacy concerns, lack of public trust, and technical complexity, ultimately leading to delays and revisions.
- Law/Standard — eIDAS Regulation (2014): While eIDAS aims to promote cross-border recognition of electronic identification, it does not mandate platform neutrality or prevent member states from relying on specific technology providers.



### Premise Attack 2 — Accountability
_Rights, oversight, jurisdiction-shopping, enforceability._

**[STRATEGIC] — Vendor Capture: The project's reliance on influencing procurement language and standards bodies merely shifts the point of failure from Apple/Google to the standards bodies and procurement processes themselves, which are equally vulnerable to capture by powerful incumbents.**

**Bottom Line:** REJECT: The project's strategy of influencing procurement and standards bodies is a high-risk, low-reward approach that merely shifts the locus of control without addressing the fundamental issue of vendor capture.


#### Reasons for Rejection

- The project's success hinges on influencing procurement and standards, which are often opaque and susceptible to lobbying, undermining the goal of genuine platform neutrality.
- By focusing on procurement language, the project risks creating a false sense of security without addressing the underlying technical dependencies and potential for vendor lock-in at a different layer.
- The project lacks a clear mechanism for ensuring ongoing accountability and oversight of the procurement and standards processes it seeks to influence, leaving it vulnerable to regulatory capture.
- The value proposition is undermined by the assumption that influencing procurement language will translate into meaningful change, ignoring the potential for superficial compliance and continued reliance on dominant platforms.

#### Second-Order Effects

- **T+0–6 months — The Honeymoon Phase:** Initial reports and demonstrators generate positive press, but fail to address the deeper architectural issues.
- **T+1–3 years — The Lobbying War:** Incumbent vendors aggressively lobby standards bodies and procurement officials, diluting or neutralizing the project's influence.
- **T+5–10 years — The Paper Tiger:** Platform-neutral language is included in procurement documents, but implemented in ways that maintain the dominance of existing platforms.
- **T+10+ years — The Status Quo:** The project's goals remain unmet, with Denmark still reliant on a small number of foreign technology suppliers for digital identity.

#### Evidence

- Law/Standard — eIDAS Regulation: The regulation itself is subject to interpretation and implementation at the national level, creating opportunities for regulatory capture.
- Case/Report — The European Ombudsman's inquiry into the European Commission's use of proprietary software: Highlights the risk of institutional dependence on specific vendors.
- Case/Report — The UK's NHS IT procurement failures: Illustrates how well-intentioned procurement policies can fail to deliver desired outcomes due to vendor influence and technical complexity.
- Narrative — Front-Page Test: A headline reads, "Denmark's Digital Identity Still Reliant on Foreign Tech Giants Despite Years of Effort."



### Premise Attack 3 — Spectrum
_Enforced breadth: distinct reasons across ethical/feasibility/governance/societal axes._

**[STRATEGIC] This project's premise is fatally flawed because it assumes that a 10.5 million DKK budget can overcome the inertia of entrenched platform dependencies and regulatory capture.**

**Bottom Line:** REJECT: The project's premise is built on a naive underestimation of the power dynamics at play, rendering its goals unattainable within the proposed budget and timeframe.


#### Reasons for Rejection

- The 10.5 million DKK budget is dwarfed by the resources of Apple and Google, making meaningful procurement intervention against their platform dominance highly improbable.
- Relying on 'resilience framing' and 'standards pressure' ignores the reality that convenience and cost often outweigh security and sovereignty concerns in public procurement decisions.
- The project's success hinges on influencing AltID, yet it lacks a concrete mechanism to ensure Digitaliseringsstyrelsen adopts its recommendations, rendering the effort largely symbolic.
- The 24-month timeline is insufficient to navigate the complex regulatory landscape and overcome the resistance of incumbent operators deeply embedded within the MitID ecosystem.
- The project's reliance on a coalition of civil-society organizations and sympathetic politicians is a weak counterweight to the lobbying power and technical expertise of Apple and Google.

#### Second-Order Effects

- 0–6 months: The project will likely produce a well-researched report and demonstrators, but these will be dismissed as impractical by decision-makers prioritizing short-term convenience.
- 1–3 years: AltID will launch with the same platform dependencies as MitID, solidifying Apple and Google's control over Danish digital identity for the foreseeable future.
- 5–10 years: Denmark will remain vulnerable to platform policy changes and geopolitical conditions, with no viable fallback authentication path for essential public services.

#### Evidence

- Case — United States v. Microsoft (1998): Demonstrated the difficulty of breaking up entrenched monopolies, even with significant legal and political pressure.
- Report — ENISA Threat Landscape for 5G Networks (2021): Highlights the risks of vendor lock-in and supply-chain vulnerabilities in critical infrastructure, mirroring the project's concerns.
- Law — European Electronic Communications Code (2018): While aiming for greater competition, its implementation often struggles against the market power of dominant platforms.



### Premise Attack 4 — Cascade
_Tracks second/third-order effects and copycat propagation._

**This project is a strategically naive attempt to dismantle a deeply entrenched technological dependency through bureaucratic maneuvering and demonstrator projects, fundamentally misunderstanding the power dynamics and inertia inherent in established digital ecosystems.**

**Bottom Line:** This project is doomed to fail because it fundamentally misunderstands the power dynamics and inertia inherent in established digital ecosystems. Abandon this quixotic quest; the premise itself is flawed, not merely the implementation.


#### Reasons for Rejection

- **The 'Resilience Theater' Trap:** Reframing the issue as 'resilience' and 'continuity' is a superficial attempt to bypass the core issue of platform lock-in, which will be easily dismissed by incumbent operators who will argue that the current system is 'good enough' and any alternative is less convenient.
- **The 'Procurement Purgatory' Paradox:** Attempting to influence procurement language is futile because the existing system is already deeply embedded in the procurement process, and any new requirements will be interpreted in a way that favors the status quo.
- **The 'Demonstrator Delusion':** Building non-production demonstrators is a waste of resources because they will be dismissed as irrelevant by decision-makers who are primarily concerned with the immediate functionality and cost-effectiveness of the existing system.
- **The 'Coalition of the Unheard':** Assembling a coalition of civil-society organizations, privacy advocates, and academic researchers is unlikely to have any real impact because these groups lack the political and economic power to challenge the established digital ecosystem.
- **The 'EU Echo Chamber' Effect:** Engaging with EU bodies and technical working groups is a distraction because these bodies are often slow-moving and ineffective, and their recommendations are unlikely to be implemented in a timely manner.

#### Second-Order Effects

- **Within 6 months:** The project will encounter significant resistance from Digitaliseringsstyrelsen and the MitID operator, who will argue that the current system is secure and reliable.
- **1-3 years:** The project will fail to secure any meaningful changes to AltID procurement or design, and the platform-neutrality language will be watered down or ignored.
- **5-10 years:** Denmark will remain heavily dependent on Apple and Google for digital identity, and the lack of a platform-neutral fallback path will create significant vulnerabilities in the event of a major platform outage or policy change.
- **Ongoing:** The project will consume significant resources without achieving any tangible results, and the failure will discourage future attempts to challenge the dominance of large technology platforms.

#### Evidence

- The UK's attempt to create a national digital identity system, Gov.uk Verify, failed spectacularly due to a combination of institutional inertia, regulatory friction, and incumbent resistance. The project consumed hundreds of millions of pounds without achieving its goals, and the UK remains heavily reliant on private-sector identity providers.
- The history of open-source software adoption in government is littered with examples of projects that failed to gain traction due to a lack of political support, bureaucratic obstacles, and the dominance of proprietary solutions. The Danish project is likely to suffer a similar fate.
- The failure of numerous attempts to break the dominance of Microsoft Windows in the desktop operating system market demonstrates the difficulty of challenging established technology platforms, even with technically superior alternatives.



### Premise Attack 5 — Escalation
_Narrative of worsening failure from cracks → amplification → reckoning._

**[STRATEGIC] — Vendor Capture: By focusing on influencing procurement language and standards rather than building a deployable alternative, the project concedes control to incumbent vendors, ensuring its recommendations are either ignored or co-opted to reinforce existing dependencies.**

**Bottom Line:** REJECT: By prioritizing policy influence over deployable solutions, the project sets itself up for co-option and irrelevance, ensuring that Denmark remains trapped in its dependence on foreign platform providers. The project's premise is fatally flawed.


#### Reasons for Rejection

- The project's reliance on influencing procurement language and standards provides a false sense of progress while allowing incumbent vendors to subtly manipulate the process to their advantage, undermining the goal of platform neutrality.
- By not creating a production-ready alternative, the project lacks the leverage to force meaningful change, as it offers no viable substitute to the current system, leaving it vulnerable to being dismissed as impractical.
- The focus on policy and advocacy dilutes the project's technical credibility, making it easier for stakeholders to dismiss the demonstrators as mere prototypes without real-world applicability or scalability.
- The project's success metrics, such as securing a formal written response or influencing procurement documents, are easily gamed and do not guarantee actual implementation of platform-neutral or fallback access mechanisms.

#### Second-Order Effects

- T+0–6 months — The Cracks Appear: Incumbent vendors offer superficial concessions in procurement language, creating the illusion of progress while maintaining their control over the underlying infrastructure.
- T+1–3 years — Copycats Arrive: Other advocacy groups and consultancies emerge, mimicking the project's approach but lacking its technical depth, further diluting the push for genuine platform neutrality.
- T+5–10 years — Norms Degrade: Platform-neutrality and fallback access become buzzwords devoid of substance, used to justify vendor lock-in under the guise of innovation and efficiency.
- T+10+ years — The Reckoning: A major service outage due to platform dependency exposes the hollowness of the project's achievements, leading to public outcry and a scramble for real solutions that should have been implemented years ago.

#### Evidence

- Case/Report — The UK's NHS attempted to introduce open standards for interoperability but faced significant resistance from incumbent vendors who prioritized their proprietary systems, resulting in limited adoption and continued vendor lock-in.
- Principle/Analogue — Regulatory Capture: The project's reliance on influencing policy and procurement creates opportunities for incumbent vendors to exert undue influence, shaping regulations and standards to favor their interests.
