
## Strengths рҹ‘Қрҹ’ӘрҹҰҫ
- Clear problem definition: Addresses a concrete public-sector risk involving supplier concentration and reduced strategic autonomy.
- Well-defined project phases: Structured approach with gated funding across three phases.
- Strong focus on policy and advocacy: Aims to influence procurement and standards rather than direct technical disruption.
- Emphasis on resilience and continuity: Frames the issue in terms of critical public-sector needs.
- Defined success criteria: Measurable outcomes related to feasibility, demonstrators, policy engagement, and procurement influence.
- Strong team composition: Includes expertise in mobile security, regulatory compliance, policy, and technical development.
- Realistic scenario planning: Accounts for institutional inertia, regulatory friction, and incumbent resistance.
- Leverages existing frameworks: Focuses on influencing AltID and eIDAS2 rather than replacing MitID directly.

## Weaknesses рҹ‘ҺрҹҳұрҹӘ«вҡ пёҸ
- Dependency on external stakeholders: Success relies on influencing Digitaliseringsstyrelsen, AltID procurement, and EU standards bodies.
- Limited direct control: Project does not aim to build a production-ready replacement for MitID.
- Potential for scope creep: Demonstrator development could expand beyond the lean, defensible scope.
- Financing uncertainty: Relies on gated funding and potential EU/private grants.
- Risk of stakeholder resistance: Incumbent vendors and existing operators may resist platform-neutral alternatives.
- Lack of a 'killer application': The project focuses on infrastructure and policy rather than a single, compelling use-case that drives user adoption. This makes it harder to generate public enthusiasm and political momentum.
- Potential for conflicting priorities within the coalition: Balancing privacy, security, and continuity concerns may be challenging.

## Opportunities рҹҢҲрҹҢҗ
- Influence AltID procurement: Shape future digital identity systems to include platform-neutral access and fallback authentication.
- Engage with EU standards: Align Danish advocacy with broader European requirements on interoperability and resilience.
- Build a strong coalition: Unite civil society, privacy advocates, researchers, and policymakers to support platform neutrality.
- Develop technical demonstrators: Provide credible evidence that secure, standards-based access models are feasible.
- Reframe the issue: Position platform neutrality as a resilience and continuity-of-service imperative.
- Secure additional funding: Leverage demonstrators and advocacy to attract EU and private grants.
- Establish Denmark as a leader in digital sovereignty: Showcase a commitment to reducing dependence on foreign technology suppliers.
- Develop a 'killer application' for platform-neutral digital identity: Identify a high-value use-case (e.g., secure access to healthcare records, streamlined tax filing, verifiable credentials for education/employment) that demonstrates the tangible benefits of platform neutrality and drives user demand. Overcome obstacles by focusing on user-centric design, addressing security concerns, and ensuring seamless integration with existing services.
